Action Verbs That Make Your Resume Stand Out in ATS

AP
ATSPro Editorial Team
|1,400 words

The verb you choose to open a resume bullet point is one of the most important stylistic decisions in your resume — and it matters to both ATS systems and human readers. Weak opening verbs like "helped," "assisted," "worked on," and "was responsible for" signal a passive, supporting role. Strong action verbs like "Led," "Architected," "Generated," and "Negotiated" signal ownership, impact, and initiative.

ATS systems also recognize and weight action verbs. Many scoring algorithms specifically look for active, impactful language as an indicator of seniority and contribution. Here's your complete guide to 100+ action verbs, organized by function.

Verbs to Avoid — Replace These

These weak verbs add no value to your bullet points and score poorly with both ATS and recruiters:

Tried toRemove this — only state what you did, not attempts

Why Verb Variety Matters

Using the same verb repeatedly (e.g., "Managed" for every bullet point) reduces readability and signals limited range of action. Aim for a variety of verbs across your experience section that reflect different types of contributions: some leadership, some building, some analytical, some collaborative. This variety signals to both ATS and recruiters that you're a multi-dimensional contributor.

Tense Rules

  • Current role: Use present tense — "Lead a team of 5 engineers..."
  • Previous roles: Use past tense — "Led a team of 5 engineers..."
  • Be consistent within each job entry
  • Never mix tenses within the same bullet point

Conclusion

Every bullet point on your resume should start with a strong action verb that immediately communicates what you did — not what you participated in, helped with, or were responsible for. Building this habit transforms not just your ATS score but the entire impression your resume makes. Review every bullet point. Find the weak verbs. Replace them.
