PDF vs DOCX — Which Resume Format is Better for ATS?

AP
ATSPro Editorial Team
|1,200 words

It's one of the most frequently debated resume questions: Should you submit your resume as a PDF or a Word document (.docx)? The conventional wisdom has shifted several times over the past decade, and the answer in 2026 is more nuanced than most blog posts suggest. The right format depends on which ATS platform the employer is using, what the job instructions say, and whether a human might receive your resume directly. Let's break it all down.

The Core Issue — What Happens to Your File

When you submit a resume through a job portal, the ATS needs to do two things: store the original file, and extract the text and data from it for searching and scoring. Both PDF and DOCX accomplish the storage part fine. The difference is in text extraction.

DOCX (Microsoft Word format) was designed for programmatic text processing. ATS systems can read a DOCX file as structured XML underneath, which makes text extraction highly reliable. The text is clearly tagged with formatting information that ATS parsers understand natively.

PDF was designed for visual presentation — preserving layout across devices and printers. PDF text extraction is a more complex technical process. Modern PDF files encode text in various ways, and while good ATS platforms handle them well, older or less sophisticated systems may struggle, especially with PDFs that use uncommon fonts, complex layouts, or embedded tables.

Advantages of PDF for Resume Submission

  • Layout preservation: PDF guarantees your resume looks identical on every device, OS, and viewer. A DOCX file might jump margins or reflow text when opened in a different version of Word.
  • Professional appearance: For roles in design, marketing, and creative fields where visual impression matters, a well-formatted PDF signals attention to design detail.
  • Prevents accidental editing: If your resume is passing through multiple hands (recruiter → hiring manager → HR), PDF prevents inadvertent modifications.
  • No font substitution: DOCX relies on the reader having the same fonts installed. PDF embeds the fonts, so the display is always correct.

Advantages of DOCX for ATS Submission

  • Better text extraction: DOCX XML structure is natively machine-readable, producing more reliable keyword extraction across all ATS platforms.
  • No scanned document risk: PDFs can accidentally be submitted as image-based files; DOCX is always text-based by nature.
  • ATS compatibility breadth: DOCX works well in every modern ATS, while PDF has variable support — especially in older Taleo and custom HR portals.
  • Recruiter flexibility: Recruiters who want to make notes or edits before forwarding your resume to hiring managers often find DOCX easier to work with.

When PDF Can Go Wrong

PDF seems safe because it "always looks right" — but this is the very trap. Looking right to human eyes and parsing correctly to an ATS are two different things. PDF parsing fails when:

  • The PDF was created by scanning a paper document (image PDF — completely unreadable)
  • The PDF uses complex multi-column layouts that confuse linear text extraction
  • Text is rendered as vector graphics rather than Unicode characters (used by some design tools)
  • The PDF was exported from Canva, Adobe Illustrator, or Photoshop — these often embed text as graphics
  • The PDF uses unusual fonts that ATS parsers can't map to standard Unicode characters

Always test your PDF: open it, try to select and copy all the text with Ctrl+A, Ctrl+C, and paste into Notepad. If the pasted text looks like your resume, it will parse fine. If you get garbage or nothing, your PDF is image-based and will fail ATS.

When to Use Each Format — Decision Framework

  • ✅ Use DOCX when: Applying through major job portals (especially Workday, Taleo, Naukri), the application instructions say .docx, or don't specify a format preference
  • ✅ Use PDF when: Sending directly to a recruiter or hiring manager via email, applying to creative or design roles, the portal explicitly accepts PDF and has modern ATS, emailing your resume to a contact at a company
  • ✅ Keep both ready at all times — One DOCX for ATS portals, one PDF for direct human sends

File Naming Best Practices

ATS systems store your resume by filename. A file named "Resume_Final_v3_UPDATED.docx" is amateur. A file named "FirstName_LastName_Resume_2026.docx" is immediately identifiable and professional. Best practices:

  • Use format: FirstName_LastName_Resume.pdf
  • Or for tailored applications: FirstName_LastName_CompanyName.pdf
  • No spaces in filenames (use underscores or hyphens)
  • No version numbers visible to the employer
  • Always include your name — never just "Resume.pdf"

Expert Recommendation

For the majority of job seekers submitting through online portals in 2026: default to DOCX unless you have a specific reason not to. DOCX has broader ATS compatibility, eliminates the risk of image-based PDF failures, and parses more reliably across the widest range of hiring platforms. Keep a polished PDF version ready for networking, direct email outreach, and any application portals where the formatting matters and PDF parsing is reliable.
