10 Resume Mistakes That Get You Rejected by ATS

AP
ATSPro Editorial Team
|1,600 words

Your resume could be disqualifying you before a single human ever reads it. ATS software is ruthless — it doesn't consider your potential, your personality, or your genuine enthusiasm for the role. It looks for specific criteria, and if your resume doesn't meet them, you're out of the running. Here are the 10 most damaging mistakes people make on ATS resumes — and exactly how to fix each one.

Mistake #1: Using Tables, Columns, or Text Boxes

1

The Problem

Multi-column resume layouts look sophisticated to human eyes but are catastrophic for ATS parsing. When an ATS encounters a two-column layout, it reads the text left-to-right across both columns simultaneously — meaning your job title in column one gets merged with your skill in column two into incomprehensible gibberish. Text boxes are even worse: most ATS systems skip them entirely, meaning anything you put in a text box simply disappears from the parsed data.

The same applies to tables. Information stored in table cells is frequently parsed in the wrong order or simply ignored by older ATS platforms.

✅ The Fix: Use a single-column layout for all ATS-submitted resumes. Save the beautiful designed version for your personal portfolio or PDF sent directly to a contact. Your ATS resume should be as plain structurally as it is in content.
2

Mistake #2: Creative or Non-Standard Section Headers

ATS systems identify sections by scanning for recognized header names. They expect headers like "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills," and "Professional Summary." If you label your experience section "Where I've Contributed" or your skills section "My Superpowers," the ATS may fail to categorize that section — and the content inside it won't be attributed to the right data fields in your candidate profile.

✅ The Fix: Use these exact headers: Professional Summary, Work Experience (or Experience), Education, Skills, Certifications, Projects, Volunteer Work. Resist creative rebranding of section names entirely.
3

Mistake #3: Missing Keywords from the Job Description

This is the #1 ATS rejection cause. If a job description says they need "experience with Salesforce CRM" and your resume says "customer relationship management software," many ATS systems will score you zero for that keyword — even if you've used Salesforce for five years. ATS matching is primarily literal, not semantic.

✅ The Fix: For every application, copy the job description into ATSPro's JD Matcher to identify missing keywords. Then add those exact terms to your resume in relevant context — your skills section, summary, or bullet points.
4

Mistake #4: Submitting an Image-Based or Scanned PDF

A scanned PDF or an image-based PDF (created by scanning a paper resume or printing to PDF from a program that embeds the text as images) is completely invisible to ATS text parsers. The ATS reads the file and extracts zero text — resulting in an effectively blank resume from the system's perspective. This is more common than people realize, especially when downloading resume templates from design sites.

✅ The Fix: Always export from a text-based source — Microsoft Word, Google Docs, or a legitimate resume builder. Test your PDF by trying to select and copy text from it with your cursor. If you can't copy any text, neither can the ATS.
5

Mistake #5: Including Graphics, Icons, and Photos

Resume photos (common in some countries), skill rating bars (e.g., ●●●○○), icons next to your contact info, progress bars for language proficiency, and embedded logos are all total nonsense to an ATS. At best they're ignored; at worst they garble the surrounding text during parsing. Skill rating bars are particularly damaging — they give ATS no information about your actual skill level and take up space that could have real keywords.

✅ The Fix: Remove all visual elements. No photos (in US/UK/Canada job markets), no icons, no rating bars, no logos. Express your skills in words. Instead of "Python ●●●●○," write "Python (5 years, expert-level, used for data analysis and automation)."
6

Mistake #6: Missing Contact Information

This might seem basic, but many resumes are missing one or more critical contact details that ATS systems are specifically designed to extract. Missing a phone number, email address, or LinkedIn URL can cause your profile to appear incomplete, which some ATS platforms penalize in scoring. More practically, even if you pass ATS, a recruiter can't contact you.

✅ The Fix: Your resume must include: full name, professional email address, phone number with country code, city and state/country, and LinkedIn profile URL. Consider adding your GitHub (for tech roles) or portfolio URL. All on the same line or clearly at the top.
7

Mistake #7: Resume is Too Short or Too Long

A one-page resume for a 10-year career looks thin and keyword-poor. A five-page resume for a 3-year career looks like you can't edit yourself. Both extremes hurt your ATS score (too few keywords in the short case) and irritate recruiters (too much noise in the long case). ATS systems don't have a hard page limit, but they do score on keyword density — too few words means too few potential keyword matches.

✅ The Fix: 1 page for 0–3 years of experience. 2 pages for 4–10 years. 3 pages maximum for very senior roles (10+ years, academic CVs, or executive positions). Fill space with substance: more detailed bullet points, quantified achievements, and a robust skills section.
8

Mistake #8: No Quantified Achievements

Bullet points like "Responsible for managing social media accounts" give ATS systems (and recruiters) almost nothing to work with. Compare to: "Grew Instagram following by 180% in 6 months and increased engagement rate from 1.2% to 4.7% through data-driven content strategy." The second version is rich with metrics that indicate real impact — and it's far more likely to contain relevant keywords too.

✅ The Fix: Add numbers to every bullet point where possible. Percentages, dollar amounts, user counts, team sizes, timeframes — any quantification makes your achievement concrete and measurable. Aim for at least 50% of your bullet points to contain a specific number.
9

Mistake #9: Using Uncommon File Formats

Submitting your resume as a .pages file (Apple Pages), .odt file (OpenDocument), or a Google Docs sharing link is essentially guaranteed to break ATS parsing. These formats either can't be opened by the ATS at all or produce significantly degraded output. Even .PDF, while widely supported, can cause issues if the application instructions specifically request .DOCX.

✅ The Fix: Always submit in the format specified by the job application instructions. If no format is specified, DOCX is the safest universal choice. Keep both a DOCX and a PDF version ready. Never submit .pages, .odt, or image files unless explicitly asked.
10

Mistake #10: Using Headers and Footers for Important Information

Some resume templates put the candidate's name, contact information, or even key section titles in the document's header and footer areas. This is a serious mistake — many ATS parsers explicitly skip the header and footer regions of Word documents, extracting only the main body text. If your name and phone number are in the document header, they may not appear in your parsed ATS profile at all.

✅ The Fix: Put all information — including your name, contact details, and every section — in the document body. Never use Word's Insert → Header/Footer feature for resume content. Type everything directly in the main body area.

Quick ATS Audit Checklist

Before submitting any resume, run this quick 5-point audit:

  • ✅ Single column layout — no tables, columns, or text boxes
  • ✅ Standard section headers used throughout
  • ✅ All contact info in the document body (not headers/footers)
  • ✅ PDF text is selectable (not image-based)
  • ✅ At least 5 keywords from the JD appear in the resume
